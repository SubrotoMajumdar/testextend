Etiology and the role of vasopressin in hypotonic hyponatremia

Hypotonic hyponatremia has multiple etiologies 1-3

Etiologies can be categorized under the 3 main volume statuses hypovolemic hyponatremia, euvolemic hyponatremia. and hypervolemic hyponatremia. Laboratory data and a physical exam will help assess patients’ volume status. The volume status will help determine the etiology of the hyponatremia.1-3

Pathogenesis of hyponatremia and the role of vasopressin

Hypotonic hyponatremia typically results from a disruption in the free water clearance from the kidneys, which is usually due to vasopressin release or renal failure.4

Vasopressin, also known as arginine vasopressin (AVP) or antidiuretic hormone (ADH), leads to renal water reabsorption. Vasopressin is regulated by osmoreceptors and baroreceptors, and is released in response to4,5:

  • Increased plasma osmolality
  • Decreased blood volume or pressure

Vasopressin mechanism of action in renal water reabsorption5-7:

  • Vasopressin binds to the G-protein–coupled vasopressin V2 receptor on the basolateral membrane of the renal collecting duct cells
  • G-protein–coupled signaling following vasopressin binding leads to activation of adenylyl cyclase causing an increase in intracellular cAMP
  • cAMP binds to protein kinase A (PKA) activating the catalytic subunits that are bound to the cytoplasmic vesicle containing aquaporin-2 (AQP2) channels
  • Activation of PKA results in phosphorylation of the AQP2 channels
  • Phosphorylation of the AQP2 channels results in the AQP2 channels translocating to the luminal membrane of the renal collecting duct cell
  • Translocation of AQP2 channels to the luminal membrane of the collecting duct cell allows water to be reabsorbed from the collecting duct
  • AQP2 channels are retrieved back into the cell by endocytosis when vasopressin is not bound to the V2 receptor

Vasopressin often plays a role in hyponatremia5,7-9

Vasopressin secretion is suppressed once serum osmolality drops below normal; however, there are 2 instances in which vasopressin secretion will be sustained or even increased despite serum hypo-osmolality. These two cases are listed below5,7:

1Hemodynamic or baroreceptor stimuli

  • Osmotic signals to suppress vasopressin secretion are overridden when the arterial baroreceptors sense a decrease in effective circulatory volume4,7,10
  • Decreases in effective circulatory volume can be due to7,10:
    • Decreased cardiac output (CO)
    • Plasma volume depletion
    • Systemic arterial vasodilation
  • Diseases that cause a decrease in effective circulatory volume include5,10:
    • Cirrhosis (systemic vasodilation)
    • High-CO heart failure (systemic vasodilation)
    • Low-CO heart failure (decreased cardiac output)
    • Sepsis (systemic vasodilation)
  • Sustained or increased vasopressin release as a result of decreased effective circulatory volume occurs by the following mechanisms5,10,11:
    1. The decreased effective circulatory volume is sensed by the arterial baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch resulting in afferent signals to the brain
    2. The afferent signals result in the release of vasopressin as well as stimulation of sympathetic efferent pathways
    3. Increased sympathetic stimulation may also lead to the release of vasopressin
    4. Increased sympathetic stimulation can result in activation of the renin-angiotensin system (RAS), which can cause the release of vasopressin

2Dysregulation of cells that secrete vasopressin

  • Vasopressin release in this cause is not caused by osmotic or hemodynamic stimuli5,7,12
  • Causes for vasopressin release include:
    • Ectopic production of vasopressin by tumors7,12
    • Stimulation vasopressin release by drugs7,12
    • A lowered threshold for vasopressin release12
    • Dysfunction of hypothalamus that results in persistent low-grade vasopressin secretion12
  • This occurs in hyponatremia caused by SIADH7
