Impact on Hospitals

The impact of hyponatremia on hospitals: longer, more costly hospital stays

Severe hospital-associated hyponatremia can result in significantly longer hospital stays1

In a prospective, observational case-controlled study of hospital in-patients1:

  • 104 patients with severe hyponatremia (serum [Na+] < 125 mEq/L)
  • 100 patients with normonatremia (serum [Na+] > 135 mEq/L)
  • Hyponatremic patients had a significantly (P < 0.005) longer hospital stay compared to normonatremic patients
    • Hyponatremia: 16 ± 12 days
    • Normonatremia: 13 ± 11 days

Increased length of hospital stay is strongly associated with the severity of hospital-acquired hyponatremia2

In a retrospective analysis of more than 50,000 hospitalizations (N=29,904) over a 7-year period2:

  • 10,354 cases of hospital-acquired hyponatremia (serum [Na+] < 138 mEq/L) developed
  • 16,992 cases of hospitalized patients with normonatremia (serum [Na+] 138-142 mEq/L)
  • Hospital-acquired hyponatremia resulted in a 64% adjusted increase in length of stay (95% CI, 60%-68%)
  • The strength of the association between length of stay and serum [Na+] increased with decreasing serum [Na+] (see table below)

The degree of hyponatremia in acute heart failure is associated with significantly longer duration of cardiac care unit (CCU) stay3

A retrospective cohort study of 499 hospitalized patients with acute heart failure3:

  • 179 patients with mild hyponatremia (MHN) (serum [Na+] 130-134 mEq/L)
  • 88 patients with moderate to severe hyponatremia (MSHN) (serum [Na+] < 130 mEq/L)
  • 232 patients with normonatremia (N) (serum [Na+] ≥135 mEq/L)
  • Mean length of stay between all three groups (N, MHN, MSHN) was significantly different (P < 0.001)
    • N: 4.9 ± 3.6 days
    • MHN: 7.7 ± 7.1 days (P < 0.05 vs N)
    • MSHN: 14.0 ± 8.9 days (P< 0.05 vs N, P< 0.05 vs MHN)

Admission hyponatremia in patients with heart failure results in longer hospital stays and higher hospital costs4

A retrospective analysis study of 115,969 patients hospitalized for acute decompensated heart failure4:

  • 18,445 (15.9%) patients had hyponatremia (serum [Na+] 131-135 mEq/L)
  • 6,117 (5.3%) patients had severe hyponatremia (serum [Na+] ≤130 mEq/L)
  • Hospital length of stay was significantly longer (P < 0.001) in patients with hyponatremia and severe hyponatremia  compared to patients with normonatremia
  • Hospitalization costs were significantly greater (P < 0.0001) in patients with hyponatremia and severe hyponatremia  compared to patients with normonatremia (see table below)

Hyponatremia on admission results in longer, more costly hospital stays, the costliest stays occurring in the more severe cases of hyponatremia5

A retrospective cohort study of patients (aged ≥18 years) with hyponatremia (serum [Na+] ≤134 mEq/L) at admission5:

  • 1,500 patients with mild to moderate hyponatremia (serum [Na+] 130-134 mEq/L)
  • 547 patients with moderate to severe hyponatremia (serum [Na+] ≤129 mEq/L)
  • Comparison cohort of 7,573 patients with normonatremia (serum [Na+] 135-145 mEq/L)
  • Patients with mild to moderate or moderate to severe hyponatremia had significantly longer and more costly hospital stays compared to patients with normonatremia

Hyponatremia results in a greater utilization of healthcare resources and increases in ICU admission and hospital readmission6

A retrospective observational study of 36,048 patients that had been discharged from 445 hospitals6:

  • 18,024 patients with a primary diagnosis of hyponatremia were matched with 18,024 patients without a primary or secondary diagnosis of hyponatremia
  • Patients with hyponatremia had a 7.6% increase in length of stay (95% CL: 6.2%, 9.1%; P< 0.001)
  • Hyponatremia resulted in a mean increase in hospital costs of 8.9% (95% CL: 7.5%, 10.4%; P< 0.001)
  • Primary diagnosis of hyponatremia was associated with an increased risk of ICU admission (OR, 1.89; 95% CL: 1.72, 2.07; P <0.001)
  • Primary diagnosis of hyponatremia was associated with an increased risk of hospital readmission within 30 days  for hyponatremia (OR, 4.76; 95% CL: 4.31, 5.26; P<0.001)

CL: confidence limit; OR: odds ratio.
